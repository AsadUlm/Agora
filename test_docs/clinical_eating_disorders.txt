CLINICAL RESEARCH ON EATING DISORDERS — SUMMARY FOR DEBATE

PSYCHIATRIC CLASSIFICATION
- Anorexia Nervosa: restriction of energy intake, intense fear of weight gain, disturbed body image. Highest mortality rate of any psychiatric disorder — 5-10% per decade, primarily cardiac arrest and suicide.
- Orthorexia: obsessive focus on "healthy" eating quality rather than quantity. Not yet in DSM-5 but clinically recognized. Often presents as socially acceptable anorexia.
- ARFID (Avoidant Restrictive Food Intake Disorder): restriction without body image distortion — often missed in young women because it lacks the "vanity" component clinicians look for.

GENETIC AND BIOLOGICAL FACTORS
- Twin studies show 50-80% heritability for anorexia nervosa (Bulik et al., 2006; Thornton et al., 2011).
- Genome-wide association studies (2019, Nature Genetics, n=72,000) identified 8 significant loci associated with anorexia — overlapping with OCD, anxiety, and perfectionism traits.
- Anorexia exists in pre-social media populations, isolated communities, and cultures with no Western media exposure — confirming it is not purely a social contagion.
- Neuroimaging shows altered activity in the insula and anterior cingulate cortex in anorexia patients — regions governing interoception (sensing internal body states).

BMI AS A FLAWED METRIC
- BMI was invented by Adolphe Quetelet in 1832 as a population-level statistical tool, never intended for individual health assessment.
- Keys' 1972 study that popularized BMI for clinical use had an all-white, all-male sample.
- BMI does not distinguish muscle from fat, does not account for age, ethnicity, or sex differences in fat distribution.
- Despite this, BMI remains the primary clinical metric triggering weight loss recommendations — directly embedding "thin = healthy" into medical practice.

MEDICAL WEIGHT BIAS
- Studies show doctors spend less time with patients classified as obese, are less likely to order diagnostic tests, and more likely to attribute unrelated symptoms to weight.
- 70% of eating disorder patients report being complimented on weight loss by a healthcare provider during active illness (NEDA survey, 2020).
- "You should lose some weight" is among the most common triggers for restrictive eating disorder onset in clinical histories.

ORTHOREXIA AND WELLNESS CULTURE
- Patients with orthorexia rarely present to treatment voluntarily — their behavior is socially rewarded as "discipline" and "health consciousness."
- Clinical distinction: healthy eating improves quality of life; orthorexia diminishes it while maintaining the appearance of virtue.
