SOCIAL MEDIA AND BODY IMAGE IN YOUNG WOMEN — RESEARCH SUMMARY

PLATFORM ALGORITHMIC EFFECTS
- Instagram's internal research (2021, leaked via Frances Haugen): 32% of teen girls said that when they felt bad about their bodies, Instagram made them feel worse. Researchers found the app was "harmful to a sizable percentage of" its youngest users.
- TikTok's recommendation algorithm routes users who engage with diet content into progressively more extreme restriction content within 30 minutes of first interaction (Center for Countering Digital Hate, 2022).
- "What I eat in a day" videos average 3.2 billion views annually on TikTok. The majority feature under 1200 calories presented as normal intake.

EATING DISORDER ONSET AGE
- Average age of anorexia onset dropped from 15-16 (pre-2012, pre-Instagram) to 12-13 by 2020 (National Eating Disorders Association).
- Hospital admissions for eating disorders in girls under 17 increased 42% between 2018 and 2022 (CDC data).
- The steepest increase coincides with the widespread adoption of front-facing cameras and image filters (2012-2016).

WELLNESS CULTURE AS COVER
- The global wellness industry is valued at $4.5 trillion (Global Wellness Institute, 2023). Diet and weight loss accounts for $260 billion of that.
- "Clean eating," "detox," and "intermittent fasting" content is algorithmically indistinguishable from pro-anorexia content in terms of behavioral outcome for vulnerable users.
- Influencers selling detox teas, appetite suppressant lollipops, and fasting apps have documented followings of 60-70% teenage girls.

PRO-ANA COMMUNITY MIGRATION
- Tumblr banned pro-anorexia content in 2012 → communities migrated to Pinterest, then Instagram, then TikTok.
- Each migration brought larger audiences. TikTok pro-restriction content reaches 10x the audience of equivalent Tumblr content at peak.

HEALTHY = THIN CONFLATION IN MEDIA
- Analysis of 100 top health and fitness Instagram accounts: 87% featured bodies in the lowest 15th percentile of healthy BMI range as the aspirational image.
- "Obesity epidemic" media coverage correlates with increased eating disorder diagnoses — moral panic about fatness normalizes extreme restriction as responsible behavior.
